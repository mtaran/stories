The session focuses on preparing and decorating a gingerbread house with Alex, his mother, and his grandmother Gigi. The mother begins by listing all the materials and tools needed: a mixer, bowl, scissors, aluminum foil, spatula, spreader, wax paper, parchment paper, green food coloring, and paper towels. Alex repeats many of these words, practicing vocabulary while helping gather supplies.

The mother washes Alex's sticky hands before they begin. They examine the pre-made gingerbread house pieces and candy decorations including ghosts, jellybeans, and candy corn. Alex is initially tempted to eat the decorative candies, not understanding that these are for decoration rather than consumption. His mother repeatedly reinforces that they are "just for pretend" and not meant to be eaten.

They review the instruction manual together and discuss the different house options available, ultimately choosing a combination of the "wacky welcome" and "creepy cottage" designs. The mother explains that this choice is because one has a cute spider and multiple ghosts to decorate the design.

The assembly begins with wrapping the gingerbread house pieces in aluminum foil. The mother guides Alex through folding and smoothing the foil, teaching him proper technique. Gigi joins in and introduces the word "pouch" when describing the ingredient bags. Alex practices saying "pouch" after being gently corrected.

Next, they prepare the icing by mixing it in a bowl and using an electric mixer. Alex watches as his mother plugs in the mixer and beats the icing on low speed, then moves to a faster speed. He finds the process interesting and cool. They prepare multiple batches of icing in different colors - orange, green, black, and white - each requiring the addition of food coloring and water.

Alex becomes increasingly excited about the project but also increasingly frustrated. He wants to taste the icing and eat the candy decorations multiple times, leading to gentle corrective interactions with his mother. He constantly asks about watching "Comfy Couch," a television show, creating a minor distraction throughout the session. His mother mentions that the TV is broken and needs batteries.

The mother demonstrates how to use a pastry bag and decorating tips by having Alex practice squeezing and twisting the bag to create frosting decorations. She shows him how bubble wrap works when they pop the protective bubble wrap from around the house pieces. Alex finds this entertaining and practices pressing and popping the bubbles.

They carefully open the gingerbread house pieces, and Alex counts them as they're unpacked. The mother shows him the white cardboard base and explains that they need to carefully fill the pastry bags with icing in the appropriate colors. Alex decides they should use black and orange icing for decorating.

Throughout the decorating process, Alex requests drinks of water and mentions being thirsty and sticky. The mother has Gigi bring him water several times. Alex also engages in some challenging behaviors - throwing things, getting frosting on his clothes, and putting his feet on the table - which his mother addresses with gentle redirection.

The session demonstrates typical toddler cooking experiences where a parent/grandparent provides structured guidance through a multi-step project while managing the child's simultaneous excitement, distraction, and desire to taste ingredients. Despite Alex's occasional frustration and behavioral challenges, his mother maintains patience and continues engaging him in the learning experience.

By the end, they have completed the preparation and are ready to decorate the gingerbread house with icing. The activity showcases vocabulary building (mixer, aluminum foil, spatula, pastry bag), following multi-step instructions, understanding pretend vs. real eating, and cooperation between child and caregivers in completing a shared project.
