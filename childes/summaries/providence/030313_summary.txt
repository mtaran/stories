The child and mother engage in bead-threading activity where the child strings colored beads on cords to make jewelry. The child decides to make a bead snake and later shifts to creating bracelets and necklaces. He selects specific color sequences and discusses which colors to add next, saying phrases like "yellow is next" and "green is next."

The mother guides the child's work by helping him organize his materials and manage the bead strings. She notices that some of the beads are broken and asks if he wants to throw them away or keep them. When the child is ready to make a necklace, the mother assists by removing beads that don't fit and helping him tie knots at the ends. She also suggests he make two necklaces instead of one.

During the activity, the child becomes imaginative, creating a "bead ladder" where beads climb up and down the strung cord. He narrates the action of a bead going up and down, adding creative storytelling to the craft. The mother responds positively to his ideas and encourages this imaginative play while maintaining the craft focus.

They work on creating patterns with the beads, with the mother explaining that matching colors creates a pattern. She suggests combining blue and yellow beads in alternating sequence. The child practices this concept, though he sometimes suggests different color combinations like blue and orange or yellow and orange. The mother approves his choices and reinforces the pattern-making concept.

Challenges arise when a bead cord breaks, and the mother explains they'll need to fix it. She helps the child manage these disappointments and continues with functional beads. The child shows persistence, collecting beads and assembling them on his cord despite setbacks.

Towards the end of the bead activity, the mother helps the child tie knots to secure the necklaces so they can be worn. The child is eager to show his completed necklaces to other family members, and the mother gives him permission to display them briefly before returning to continue working.

They then transition to playing memory games, setting up cards in organized rows so they can play matching games together. The mother teaches the rules about turning cards over and finding matching pairs. This activity stretches for a substantial portion of the session, with the mother and child taking turns and discussing the matches they find.
